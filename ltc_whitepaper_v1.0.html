Mathematical Abstractions of the UPS Sort Training System.

A formal account of sort space, the routing function, operator complexity, and tier-transition dynamics — with the failure modes that surfaced during validation.

// abstract
The UPS sort floor is a metric space in disguise. This document treats it as one: positions become points, the routing function $R$ becomes a map between them, and operator complexity becomes a measure on top. From that structure, tier transitions, mastery curves, and failure modes all fall out — and several long-standing operational frustrations resolve as predictions of the model rather than anomalies in the data.
/ 01

Sort space as a metric space

1.1 — Positions, points, distance

Let $S$ denote the set of belt positions on the floor, $|S| = 184$ at CHEMA. Each $s \in S$ has a fixed physical location and a fixed set of valid destination slots $D(s) \subseteq D$. We define the routing distance between positions $s_i, s_j$ as the minimum number of slot rebinds needed for the operator at $s_i$ to take over $s_j$'s workload without losing routing fidelity:

// eq. 1.1 — routing distance $$ d(s_i, s_j) \;=\; \big|\, D(s_i) \,\triangle\, D(s_j) \,\big| \;+\; \mathbb{1}[\,z(s_i) \neq z(s_j)\,]\cdot\kappa $$

The first term is the symmetric difference of slot sets — what the operator must learn or forget. The second is a fixed cost when $s_i$ and $s_j$ are in different zones, scaled by the empirically derived constant $\kappa$. The metric is well-defined: symmetry and the triangle inequality both hold; proof in Appendix A.

1.2 — The routing function and what it forgets

The routing function $R: S \to \mathcal{P}(D)$ formalizes what a position is for. It is not injective — many positions share destination sets — and not surjective onto $\mathcal{P}(D)$. R is what training certifies; it is also what training keeps incomplete, because the field operation routinely rebinds slots without updating the trainer's reference. That update lag is one of the failure modes treated in §6.

// open problem · OP-1

Is $d$ a true metric on $S \cup \{\,\text{borrowed positions}\,\}$? Borrow events break the static $D(s)$ assumption — every borrow temporarily re-defines the destination set. A time-indexed extension $d_t$ is sketched in §3.4 but the triangle inequality has not been proven under realistic borrow density.

The implication for training is direct: any certification that does not track R's drift will over-state mastery on positions whose $D(s)$ has rebound since the cohort last certified. Section 4 formalizes how often this happens; §6 documents the corrections currently in place.

/ 02

The routing function $R$ — structure and pathologies

The routing function $R: S \to \mathcal{P}(D)$ is what the sort training platform certifies sorters against. This section formalizes its structure, identifies the operational pathologies that arise from $R$'s incompleteness, and shows that several long-standing "data fidelity" problems are in fact properties of $R$ rather than bugs in the data.

The full development of §2.1 through §2.4 — surjectivity bounds, the pre-image structure of confusable belt clusters, and the relationship between $|R^{-1}(D')|$ and per-belt error rate — continues in the body of the original whitepaper.
